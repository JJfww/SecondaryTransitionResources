Secondary Transition Resource Guide

Overview

This guide is designed to assist secondary special education teachers in supporting students through the critical transition period from high school to postsecondary life. It covers a wide range of topics including curriculum, transition assessments, and community resources. The aim is to provide teachers with practical tools and strategies to help their students succeed in the next phase of their lives. This is a living document, meaning things can be added or removed as new resources are found or old resources are no longer relevevant. Please share anything you think should be included with Jennifer Burnes.

The transition from high school to post-secondary life is a critical period for students with disabilities. It can be a time of great uncertainty and anxiety, as they navigate the challenges of finding employment, pursuing higher education, or living independently. As a special education teacher, you play a crucial role in supporting your students through this transition. This guide is designed to provide you with a comprehensive set of resources and strategies to help your students succeed. From assessments to curriculum and community resources, this guide will equip you with the tools you need to help your students navigate this important period in their lives.

As you build your schedule, keep in mind the importance of routines. Routines and structure serve as a stable foundation for many of our students, offering a sense of predictability and security. Monthly Themes

The monthly themes in our curriculum section cover a range of relevant topics for young adults transitioning to adulthood. Each theme provides an opportunity for additional focus on specific areas, but the topics can also be integrated throughout the year as needed.

For example, in August, our focus is on personal preferences and interests, as well as team building activities. This is an important time for our students to explore their passions and learn more about themselves, while also developing important social and communication skills. In November, more focus will be given to important social skills. This theme is designed to help our students develop the skills they need to navigate social situations and build positive relationships with others.

Other themes throughout the year include topics such as personal safety, sex education, self-advocacy, and community involvement. Each theme is designed to provide a comprehensive and engaging learning experience that will help our students prepare for the challenges and opportunities that await them after they exit the school system.

By focusing on these themes throughout the year, we are able to provide a consistent and meaningful educational experience for our students, while also creating opportunities for continued growth and development. What we learn about our students during these themes can carry over throughout the year, providing a strong foundation for success.

Person Centered Planning tools directs you to the LifeCourse Library's section on Person-Centered Foundational Tools. LifeCourse Library is a resource hub that offers tools, materials, and resources to support individuals with disabilities and their families in achieving their desired outcomes across the lifespan.

The Person-Centered Foundational Tools are designed to help individuals with disabilities, their families, and professionals create person-centered plans that take into account the individual's strengths, preferences, and needs. These tools are meant to empower people to take control of their lives and to ensure that services and supports are tailored to meet their unique requirements.

The tools in this section are organized into the following categories:

  1. Charting the LifeCourse Framework: A collection of tools and resources to help individuals and families develop a vision for a good life, identify the steps needed to achieve it, and understand the different types of supports available.

  2. Person-Centered Thinking and Planning: Tools and resources that support the use of person-centered thinking and planning processes, which focus on the individual's unique strengths, preferences, and needs.

  3. Conversations on the Journey: Resources to help facilitate conversations about the person's life journey, including their past experiences, present situation, and future goals.

  4. Implementation and Monitoring: Tools to support the implementation of person-centered plans and monitor progress toward desired outcomes.

  5. Capacity Building: Resources to help individuals, families, and professionals develop the skills and knowledge needed to create and sustain person-centered plans and supports.

Circles of Friends/Support is a person-centered approach to building support networks for individuals with disabilities. This platform offers resources and information on inclusive practices, person-centered planning, and community building for people with disabilities.

Circles of Friends is a strategy that involves creating a support network of friends, family, professionals, and community members who come together to help an individual with disabilities achieve their goals and aspirations. The main purpose of Circles of Friends is to promote social inclusion and ensure that individuals with disabilities have the opportunity to build meaningful relationships and participate fully in their community.

The page provides the following information on Circles of Friends:

  1. Introduction: A brief explanation of the Circles of Friends concept and its benefits.
  2. Stories and Examples: Real-life stories and examples of how Circles of Friends have positively impacted the lives of individuals with disabilities.
  3. Articles and Publications: A list of articles and publications that discuss the Circles of Friends approach and provide guidance on how to implement it.
  4. Workshops and Training: Information on workshops and training sessions offered by Inclusion.com to help families, educators, and support workers develop the skills and knowledge needed to create and maintain Circles of Friends.
  5. Videos: A selection of videos showcasing stories of Circles of Friends and the positive impact they have on the lives of individuals with disabilities.

Optional Icebreaker Activities

Modify/adapt to meet your students' needs. Include your paras in the activities!

  1. Sensory scavenger hunt: Create a list of items with different sensory characteristics (e.g., soft, smooth, rough, warm, cold) that can be found in the environment. Participants work in pairs or small groups to find and collect these items. This activity encourages communication and teamwork while engaging various senses.
  2. The name game: Each participant introduces themselves and shares a word or phrase that starts with the same letter as their name (e.g., "Friendly Fred"). As each person introduces themselves, the group must repeat the previous names and adjectives. This activity helps participants remember each other's names and encourages listening skills.
  3. Collaborative art project: Participants work together to create a group art piece, such as a mural or collage. Encourage them to communicate their ideas and preferences, either verbally or non-verbally. This activity fosters teamwork, creativity, and self-expression.
  4. Two truths and a lie: Each participant shares three statements about themselves – two truths and one lie. The rest of the group tries to guess which statement is the lie. This activity encourages sharing and helps participants learn more about each other.
  5. Would you rather: Participants take turns asking "Would you rather" questions (e.g., "Would you rather be able to fly or be invisible?"). Everyone answers the question, and it can spark interesting discussions and help the group learn more about each other's preferences.
  6. Common ground: Participants are given a list of topics (e.g., favorite movies, hobbies, travel destinations) and must find things they have in common with others in the group. This activity encourages communication and helps participants find shared interests.
  7. Find someone who: Create a list of statements such as "Find someone who has a pet," "Find someone who has traveled to another country," or "Find someone who plays a musical instrument." Participants must find someone in the group who matches each statement and write their names down. This activity encourages mingling and conversation.

Student Led Individualized Education Programs (IEPs)

Why should we teach our students to lead their own IEPs?

Student-led IEPs are important for students with disabilities for several reasons:

  1. Empowerment and self-advocacy: Student-led IEPs involve students in the decision-making process, encouraging them to advocate for their own needs and preferences. This empowers them to take ownership of their education and builds self-confidence.

  2. Self-awareness: Participating in the IEP process helps students with disabilities develop a better understanding of their strengths, challenges, and accommodations. This self-awareness enables them to be more effective in their learning and to communicate their needs more effectively.

  3. Goal-setting and self-determination: Student-led IEPs help students set their own goals and develop strategies to achieve them. This promotes self-determination and encourages students to take responsibility for their progress.

  4. Enhanced communication: Involving students in the IEP process promotes open communication between students, parents, teachers, and other support staff. This collaboration helps to ensure that everyone is working together to meet the student's needs.

  5. Greater engagement: When students are actively involved in their IEP, they are more likely to be engaged and invested in their education. This leads to better academic performance and improved social-emotional outcomes.

  6. Transition planning: Student-led IEPs help prepare students for post-secondary education, employment, and independent living. By involving students in the planning process, they are better prepared to make decisions about their future and navigate the challenges that may arise.

  7. Personalized learning: Student-led IEPs prioritize the student's individual needs, ensuring that their education is tailored to their unique strengths and challenges. This personalized approach helps to maximize their potential and promote academic success.

In summary, student-led IEPs are crucial for students with disabilities because they empower them, encourage self-advocacy, and promote self-awareness, goal-setting, and self-determination. Additionally, these IEPs foster communication, engagement, and personalized learning, all while preparing students for the future.

Below are some resources to help you help your students lead their IEPs.

How to Help Students Lead Their IEP Meetings is a great article from Teaching Exceptional Children that outlines steps you can take to implement student led IEPs in your practice.

How to Make Student-Led IEPs a Reality is a resource from the Virginia Department of Education's Training and Technical Assistance Center. It's short, but highlights important steps you can take to incorporate student led IEPs with your students.

I'm Determined is a wonderful resource that had video modules, articles, and templates.

Transition Assessments

Here, you will find information on available transition assessments. The purpose is to help teachers access simple tools that allow them to capture transition-related services and activities happening in the classroom, during community-based instruction, and throughout a student's school year. Authentic assessments can help inform transition and transition IEP decision-making.

The resource will cover a variety of assessments, including parent surveys, education, self-determination, independent living, and employment/vocational. These assessments are designed to be simple tools that can be used to gather information on a student's transition-related skills and abilities. By gathering information from multiple settings, teachers can help ensure that their students are prepared for a wide range of post-secondary options.

One key benefit of using authentic assessments is that they help provide a more complete picture of a student's strengths and areas for improvement. By gathering information from multiple sources, such as parents, teachers, and the students themselves, teachers can gain a better understanding of the student's abilities and needs. By using authentic assessments, teachers and IEP teams can make more informed decisions about transition planning and help ensure that students are prepared for success after graduation.

**Quick Tips: IEP Meeting 

  1. Contact parents for available times and dates 

  2. Identify IEP team members 

  3. Create a Doodle to get times and dates that work for most 

  4. Send out outlook invite/email 

  5. Student canva or presentation 

  6. Send home NOM 10 days in advance 

  7. Finalize former progress reports 

  8. Collect input from Gen ed teachers and gather baseline data for new goals 

  9. With input from team, write new IEP, PWN if needed 

  10. Send home IEP draft within 5 days (do not include PWN) 

  11. Get confirmation of who can and cannot attend meeting

  12. Appoint meeting notes “taker” 

  13. Possibly: Gen ed excusal form 

  14. Decide how you are going to show the IEP at the meeting/project or print off 

  15. Get a copy of parental rights (school pysch. Will have this) 

  16. Student involvement - how is the student involved in their IEP 

  17. Create/use IEP Meeting Agenda 

  18. Have IEP meeting, PWN, NOM, Excusal signed (all if needed). Double check that someone is taking notes 

  19. Attach signed paperwork and possible other needed forms 

  20. Finalize IEP and send home final copy to parents**
